For Partners

Alting provides governance infrastructure for organizations where members need a real voice. If your community already shares resources and makes collective decisions, Alting makes that process transparent, democratic, and scalable.

What Integration Provides

The governance layer your organization is missing.

Credentialed Membership

Verified member identity through sovereign Digital DNA. Members prove eligibility without revealing personal data. Credentials are scoped — a shareholder credential grants water company voting rights; a residency credential grants zone governance rights.

1-Person-1-Vote Governance

Democratic decision-making where every member has equal voice. Proposals go through structured deliberation before voting. Dissent is surfaced and addressed, not suppressed. All decisions recorded transparently.

Transparent Records

Every proposal, deliberation, vote, and allocation is visible to all members. Funds are traceable. Decision histories are permanent. Accountability is structural, not aspirational.

Outcome Metrics

Community-defined indicators — belonging, safety, financial security, environmental stewardship — measured quarterly through anonymous member input. Results inform decisions without ranking or scoring communities.

Federation

Connect with other Tings for cross-community coordination, collective bargaining, and shared learning — without surrendering local autonomy. No higher layer can override a Ting's internal decisions.

Post-Quantum Security

Every layer — identity, communication, governance, voting, transactions — secured with encryption designed to withstand quantum computing. The governance record is incorruptible for generations.

Who This Is For

Organizations where members deserve a vote.

Water Companies & Utilities

Mutual water companies, cooperative utilities, and member-owned infrastructure. Shareholders get transparent governance over rates, capital investment, emergency response, and resource allocation. Las Flores Water Co. is the working prototype.

Housing & Land Trusts

Community land trusts, housing cooperatives, and residential developments with shared stewardship. Residents govern shared resources — from woodland management to community programming — with real democratic authority.

Community Organizations

Neighborhood associations, business corridor collectives, mutual aid networks, and civic groups. Any organization where people come together around shared interests and need transparent, accountable decision-making.

How It Works

Three phases to self-governance.

Phase 1

Pilot Ting

Start with a small group — five households, a single board, one committee. Stand up a Ting with real members making real decisions. Define membership criteria, decision domains, and initial priorities. Prove the model works in your context before scaling.

Phase 2

Template Integration

Package the governance framework as part of your organization's operating model. Define the Ting template: who participates, what they decide, how budgets are allocated, how conflicts are resolved. Make democratic governance part of what members receive.

Phase 3

Federation

As your organization grows — more communities, more locations, more members — Alting federates them. Each Ting governs locally. Cross-community coordination happens through the federation layer. Scaling without centralizing.

What is a Ting?

A Ting is any organization people govern together — a block group, a water company, a land trust, a business corridor. Members participate as equals, make decisions transparently, and build trust through structured collaboration.

Alting — Petra Wennberg. Altadena, California.
