In Practice

Alting is not theoretical. These are real communities at different stages of adopting self-governance infrastructure — from active deployment to early exploration.

Active

Altagether

Post-fire neighborhood governance, Altadena, California

After the Eaton Fire devastated Altadena in January 2025, the community faced a choice: let outside capital and institutions dictate recovery, or build community-owned systems that keep resources local and decisions democratic.

Altagether is the bottom-up answer. Organized into 17 zones with volunteer block captains, it gives residents a real voice in how their neighborhood rebuilds — who decides what gets rebuilt, and whether every person has a say.

Zone 129 serves as the pilot — the first zone to formalize democratic governance through the Alting framework, covering Morada Place, Mar Vista Avenue, and New York Drive. What works here becomes the template for every zone.

Las Flores Water Co.

Mutual water company shareholder governance

Las Flores Water Company is a mutual water company — owned by its shareholders, not by investors. Every household that receives water holds a share and has a right to participate in how the company is governed.

The Alting framework gives Las Flores a transparent governance layer: proposals for infrastructure investment, rate changes, and emergency response are deliberated openly. Every shareholder gets one vote. Every decision is recorded.

This is a working prototype — a live Ting template that demonstrates how Alting governance applies to shared utility infrastructure. The pattern scales to any mutual water company, any cooperative utility, any member-owned organization.

Mariposa Junction

Commercial corridor co-design, Altadena

Mariposa Junction brings together local businesses and residents along Altadena's commercial corridors to co-design the future of their shared neighborhood spaces. Rather than top-down zoning decisions made without community input, Mariposa Junction uses the Ting framework to give every stakeholder — business owner and resident alike — a structured voice.

Currently in formation, this Ting is defining its membership criteria, decision domains, and initial priorities. The goal: a commercial corridor where decisions about development, public space, and local economy are made by the people who live and work there.

Exploring

NeighbourWood

First European Ting prospect — Ireland & UK

NeighbourWood builds car-free, woodland-integrated residential communities across Ireland and the UK. Each community already has the ingredients of a Ting: credentialed members (homeowners), shared resources (woodland, Village Green, solar canopy), and collective decision-making through community AGMs.

What's missing is the digital governance layer that makes resident self-governance real, transparent, and scalable across every future NeighbourWood community. Alting would become the governance module within NeighbourWood's operating system — turning homeowners into co-stewards with a real vote in how stewardship funds are allocated, how shared spaces are managed, and how communities federate as the network grows.

This would be the first Ting outside the United States — proof that the framework translates across cultures and contexts.

What is a Ting?

A Ting is any organization people govern together — a block group, a water company, a land trust, a business corridor. Members participate as equals, make decisions transparently, and build trust through structured collaboration.
