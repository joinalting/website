The opportunity

NeighbourWood builds homes inside nature. But homes are not a community.

The 10-homes-per-hectare model creates proximity. Native woodland creates shared stewardship. But what creates collaboration?

What do residents use to make decisions together? To manage shared resources? To coordinate planting days, resolve disagreements, and build the trust that turns ten households into a neighborhood?

The physical infrastructure is extraordinary. The community infrastructure doesn’t exist yet.

Current digital tools were not designed for co-living. They measure attention, not stewardship. They reward loudness, not deliberation. A NeighbourWood community needs infrastructure built for how neighbors actually live together.

The deeper point

The tools shape the community

WhatsApp groups fragment information. Email threads lose context. Facebook rewards engagement, not consensus. The medium is the governance.

When ten households share woodland and common spaces, the tools they use to communicate are not neutral. They shape whether disagreements escalate or resolve. Whether decisions stick or unravel. Whether trust builds or erodes.

Alting’s tools are designed so that using them is the training. Transparent proposals teach people to propose clearly. Structured deliberation teaches people to listen. Shared dashboards keep everyone focused on what matters.

The alternative

Alting

Infrastructure for how people organize, collaborate, and govern together — built from the bottom up.

Each community organization is called a Ting. A Ting can be a neighborhood, a co-living community, a water company, a land trust. Each Ting governs itself. Multiple Tings federate — sharing infrastructure, never overriding each other.

A NeighbourWood community is a natural Ting — 10 households sharing land, woodland, and a stewardship commitment.

The smallest unit is a person. Every design decision flows from that. One person, one vote. Identity belongs to the person, not the platform. Consensus is tied to personhood, not tokens.

What it looks like

A NeighbourWood Ting

Three pillars of shared life — all governed through the same transparent infrastructure.

Community Governance

Proposals, structured deliberation, and voting on shared decisions. From communal space rules to annual budgets — every household has an equal voice.

Shared Resources

Tool libraries, communal space bookings, seasonal maintenance schedules. Transparent allocation of shared assets so nothing falls through the cracks.

Woodland Stewardship

Planting day coordination, wildlife surveys, stewardship tracking. The Woodland Bank held in trust — with governance that matches the commitment.

Scaling

Federation: The NeighbourWood Network

As communities multiply, Alting connects them. Each community self-governs; the network coordinates.

Every NeighbourWood development is a Ting. As N-OS scales to new sites, each community gets its own governance — and plugs into the wider network automatically. No central authority overrides a local decision.

  • Cross-community dashboards: Aggregated priorities, shared learning, and network-wide stewardship metrics across all NeighbourWood sites.
  • Shared credentials: A resident credential that works in your own community Ting and across the network. Prove once, participate everywhere.
  • Knowledge sharing: What one community learns about woodland management, tool libraries, or conflict resolution becomes available to every community.
  • Collective purchasing: Coordinated sourcing of native saplings, shared tools, and maintenance services — economies of scale without loss of autonomy.

Value that stays

NeighbourWood Credits

A local currency earned through stewardship labor, spent within the network.

Residents earn credits by contributing to the commons — planting days, wildlife surveys, tool library maintenance, mentoring new households. Credits are spent within the NeighbourWood network: on shared services, communal space bookings, or seasonal supplies.

  • Earned through contribution — not purchased. Stewardship work generates value.
  • Non-convertible — value cannot be extracted from the community. Credits circulate; they don’t leave.
  • Contribution-based — holding more credits gives no additional governance power. Voice stays equal.
  • Network-wide — credits earned in one NeighbourWood community are recognized across the network.

The economy reflects the values: contribution, not consumption, is the source of standing.

Architecture

Load-bearing walls

Five structural commitments that prevent the system from being captured or hollowed out. Remove any one and the model is vulnerable.

  • 1-person-1-vote No one buys more voice. Every household member has equal standing.
  • Non-convertible currency Value cannot be extracted. Credits circulate, never leave.
  • Community land trust The Woodland Bank is held in trust. Land cannot be speculated on.
  • Member-owned commons Shared resources belong to the community, not a platform.
  • Bidirectional transparency The provider is as visible as the community. No asymmetric information.

Together: structurally un-exploitable. The architecture guarantees it — not just the good intentions of the people running it.

Where we are

Three active Tings in Altadena, California

The same infrastructure, a different context. Post-fire community rebuilding — neighbors already governing together.

After the Eaton Fire destroyed 9,000+ structures, the grassroots block captain network Altagether organized 130+ zones. Three Tings are already operating:

  • Altagether Ting — Residential zones making shared decisions on infrastructure reconstruction, utility undergrounding, and road priorities.
  • Las Flores Water Co. Ting — A 140-year-old mutual water company with 1,400 shareholders. Working prototype for transparent governance and secure voting.
  • Mariposa Junction Ting — Commercial corridor co-design where residents and merchants decide together where trees, bioswales, and streetscape elements go.

Same tools. Same process. Different context. NeighbourWood would be the first European Ting.
